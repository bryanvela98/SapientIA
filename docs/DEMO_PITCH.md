# SapientIA — 3-minute demo pitch

A non-technical, high-impact walk-through aimed at hackathon judges or
a general audience. Leads with the differentiator (refuses to give
answers), shows accessibility as a *first-class* concept rather than a
coat of paint, ends on the voice-control moment that makes the room
go quiet.

Total runtime: **2:50–3:00**. Six scenes, one cold open.

---

## The pitch in one line

> *"Every AI tutor gives you the answer. SapientIA refuses to — and
> teaches you to think for yourself, on your terms, no matter how you
> learn."*

---

## Scene-by-scene script

Times are anchor marks for editing. Voiceover word counts are tuned to
~150 words/minute (deliberate, professorial pacing — not ad-copy
energy).

### COLD OPEN — 0:00–0:15 (the problem)

**On screen:**
- 3-up split: a generic AI chat with "What's a derivative?" → wall of
  text answer. Three logos, one answer each. Cursor highlights the
  fact that they all just *gave* the answer.
- Cut to the SapientIA chat with the same question — the tutor
  replies with a question instead.

**Voiceover:**

> Every AI tutor today does the same thing. You ask. It answers. The
> student gets a fish — and never learns to fish.

**B-roll capture list:** see Capture A (cold-open split) below.

---

### SCENE 1 — 0:15–0:45 (the Socratic hook + earned-vs-told)

**On screen:**
- Full-screen SapientIA chat. Topic: "limits in calculus" or whatever
  is recognizable.
- The learner answers; tutor responds with a hint, then another
  question.
- **Concept badges in the header light up: "earned 1 / told 0".**
  Pause briefly so the badges read.
- After the next learner reply: another earned concept lights.

**Voiceover:**

> Meet SapientIA. It refuses to give the answer. Instead, it asks
> better questions — guiding you to discover the concept yourself.
>
> Watch the counter. Every idea you *reason through* becomes "earned."
> Every idea you're handed becomes "told." Earned concepts stick.
> Told concepts evaporate. SapientIA is the first tutor that measures
> the difference.

**B-roll capture list:** Capture B.

---

### SCENE 2 — 0:45–1:15 (accessibility changes how the AI thinks)

**On screen:**
- Cut to the onboarding screen showing the four profile dimensions:
  Visual, Cognitive, Learning, Pacing.
- Hover/select each one briefly so all four labels register.
- Switch **Cognitive** to "Plain language."
- Cut back to the chat — the next tutor turn is visibly **shorter and
  simpler**. Hold on the bubble for ~2 seconds.

**Voiceover:**

> Most apps treat accessibility as a coat of paint — bigger fonts,
> darker mode. SapientIA goes deeper: it changes how the AI itself
> thinks.
>
> Switch on plain language. The tutor doesn't just shrink the font.
> It rewrites itself. Grade-five vocabulary. Three short sentences.
> No jargon.

**B-roll capture list:** Capture C.

---

### SCENE 3 — 1:15–1:50 (dyslexia + ADHD-focus)

**On screen:**
- Toggle **Learning → Dyslexia-friendly**. The page font visibly
  swaps to Atkinson Hyperlegible. Hold for 1 second.
- Toggle **Learning → ADHD focus**. Older transcript bubbles fade to
  ~55% opacity. Hold for 1 second.
- Press **Shift+M** — the entire header chrome and side controls
  collapse. Just title, transcript, composer left.
- Press **Shift+M** again — chrome returns.

**Voiceover:**

> A research-backed dyslexia font, designed by the Braille Institute.
> An ADHD-focus mode that fades older messages so only what matters
> right now is sharp. And one keystroke — Shift M — that strips the
> whole interface down to nothing but the conversation.
>
> No ceremony. No noise. Just learning.

**B-roll capture list:** Capture D.

---

### SCENE 4 — 1:50–2:25 (the voice command moment — the wow shot)

**On screen — this is the money beat:**
- **Hands visibly lift off the keyboard.** Camera (or a small
  picture-in-picture inset) shows the person leaning back, hands in
  lap or visibly off the desk.
- They press and hold Shift+V (announce it on screen with a small
  caption: "*holding Shift+V*").
- They say **"recap."**
- The voice-command banner appears, then fades. The recap bubble
  renders on screen with the concepts list.
- They say **"slow down."** A small toast or pacing toggle visibly
  flips.
- They say **"minimize."** Chrome collapses.

**Voiceover:**

> For learners who cannot use a keyboard or a mouse — SapientIA also
> drives by voice.
>
> Hold one key. Say "recap" — get a summary. Say "slow down" — the
> tutor adapts. Say "minimize" — the interface clears.
>
> Nine simple commands. No menus. No setup. Just speak.

**B-roll capture list:** Capture E. **This is the most important
shot in the demo.** Rehearse it. Re-record it until it lands.

---

### SCENE 5 — 2:25–2:50 (the stacked-profile proof)

**On screen:**
- Onboarding screen with **all four axes set to their accessibility-
  active value**: Visual = Screen Reader, Cognitive = Plain Language,
  Learning = Dyslexia-Friendly + ADHD-Focus (use one or hop between),
  Pacing = Slow.
- Cut to the chat. Audio: VoiceOver (or NVDA) announces the live
  region — let one announcement land audibly. Atkinson font visible.
  Older bubbles faded. Tutor turns short.
- Hold the wide shot for 3 seconds so the audience reads the
  composition.

**Voiceover:**

> And every layer stacks. A blind, dyslexic student with ADHD,
> learning their first calculus concept — every accessibility profile
> active at once, and the tutor still teaches Socratically through
> all of them.
>
> Because that's what real inclusion looks like.

**B-roll capture list:** Capture F.

---

### CLOSE — 2:50–3:00 (one-line outro)

**On screen:**
- The SapientIA wordmark or simple title card.
- Beneath it, a tiny row of four icons (eye, brain, book, microphone)
  representing the four layers.
- "Built with Claude Opus 4.7" tagline.

**Voiceover:**

> SapientIA. Powered by Claude Opus 4.7. Teaching the way humans
> actually learn — for everyone.

## Voice generation tool recommendations

The voiceover can be recorded by you, or generated with AI TTS. For a
hackathon submission, AI TTS gives you a polished, repeatable take
with no studio.

**Top pick — ElevenLabs** (paid, best quality)
- Most natural pacing and inflection. Can hold "documentary narrator"
  energy for the full 3 minutes.
- Free tier covers ~10 minutes of audio per month — enough for this
  demo plus three retakes.
- Recommended voices: **Brian** (warm, professorial) or **Charlotte**
  (calm, measured). Avoid the high-energy ad voices.
- Settings: stability ~50, similarity ~75, style exaggeration ~0–10.
- URL: elevenlabs.io

**Budget pick — OpenAI TTS** (`tts-1-hd`)
- ~$0.030 per 1K characters. Your full script is ~550 words ≈ 3300
  characters = ~$0.10 for the whole voiceover.
- Voices: `nova` (warm, female) or `onyx` (deep, male) for narration;
  `alloy` for neutral.
- Quality is good; ElevenLabs still wins on micro-pacing.
- URL: platform.openai.com

**One-stop shop — Descript** (paid, generous free tier)
- Combines TTS, screen recording, and video editing in one app. You
  can type the script, pick an "Overdub" voice, drop screen captures
  alongside the audio, and export.
- Best if you do not already have an editing workflow.
- URL: descript.com

**Skip-it pick — macOS `say` command**
- Free, instant, but the voices are noticeably synthetic and will
  date the demo. Use only as a placeholder while editing.

**If you record yourself:**
- Quiet room, soft surfaces (close the door, sit on a bed if needed).
- Use a halfway decent USB mic — Blue Yeti, FiFine K669, even
  AirPods sound usable in a quiet room.
- Record at 48 kHz / 16-bit. Save as WAV, not MP3, while editing.
- Light noise reduction in iMovie / Descript / Audacity is fine. Do
  not over-process.
- Read the script *slowly*. The instinct in a 3-minute demo is to
  rush. Resist. The Socratic concept needs the audience to absorb it
  on first hearing.

## Screen-capture (b-roll) shot list

Each capture is a short clip you record once and trim into the final
edit. Record at **1280×800 or 1920×1080** at 30fps minimum. Frame the
browser window so the SapientIA UI fills it cleanly — close DevTools,
hide the bookmarks bar, use a clean desktop wallpaper or a solid
background.

### Capture A — Cold open: "AI tutors give answers" (0:00–0:15)

- Open ChatGPT (or Claude.ai or Gemini) in three tabs side by side.
  Ask each: "What is a derivative?" — let each render its full answer.
- Optional editing trick: blur the logos slightly, since you do not
  need to single out competitors. The point lands either way.
- Final third of the clip: switch to SapientIA showing the same
  question and the tutor responding with another question.
- Total runtime ~10 seconds. Snappy cuts every 2 seconds.

### Capture B — Socratic loop + earned-vs-told badges (0:15–0:45)

- Pre-record a clean session on a recognizable topic. Use **plain
  default profile** so the focus is on the badges, not on a11y.
- Drive the conversation until **two concepts earn**. Pause after
  each badge updates so the camera can read it.
- 25–30 second clip. May need 2–3 takes; sessions are quick.
- **Pro tip:** type your learner replies *slowly* and deliberately —
  the audience needs to read along.

### Capture C — Profile picker → plain-language rewrites (0:45–1:15)

- Two clips, cut together.
- **Clip C1 (8 seconds):** the `/onboarding` page. Cursor moves over
  each fieldset. Briefly hover Visual, Cognitive, Learning, Pacing.
  No clicks; just shows that all four exist.
- **Clip C2 (15 seconds):** start a fresh chat with default profile.
  Send a question, capture the tutor turn (long, dense). Cut. Switch
  to Cognitive = Plain Language. Same question to a fresh session.
  Capture the tutor turn (visibly shorter). Hold on the second bubble.
- The cut from "before" to "after" is the entire visual argument —
  edit it tight.

### Capture D — Dyslexia font + ADHD fade + Shift+M chord (1:15–1:50)

- One continuous clip preferred (no cuts).
- Start with default profile, mid-session, ~6 turns of transcript
  visible.
- **Toggle dyslexia-friendly:** screen-record the font swap. Atkinson
  is visibly different from system-ui — hold 1 second.
- **Toggle ADHD focus:** screen-record the older bubbles fading.
  Hold 1 second.
- **Press Shift+M:** chrome collapses. Hold 1 second.
- **Press Shift+M again:** chrome returns.
- Total ~25 seconds. The fewer cuts, the more impressive — judges
  see it is one continuous app, not three apps stitched.

### Capture E — Voice commands (1:50–2:25) **THE MONEY SHOT**

- Use **picture-in-picture**: small webcam inset showing your face
  and hands, main frame showing the SapientIA UI. Or just a wide
  shot showing keyboard + screen with hands visibly off the keys.
- **Hands lift off keyboard.** Lean back. Press and hold Shift+V.
- Caption on screen (added in post): "Holding Shift+V"
- Say **"recap"** clearly. Banner appears, fades. Recap bubble
  renders. **Hold for 2 seconds** so the audience reads the recap.
- Without releasing the camera, hold Shift+V again.
- Say **"slow down."** Pacing toggle flips visibly (small toast or
  the toggle Switch animates).
- Hold Shift+V.
- Say **"minimize."** Chrome collapses.
- 30–35 second clip. Re-record this one until it is *clean*. No
  fumbles, no "uhh." If your microphone is noisy, voice the commands
  on a separate audio track and lip-sync the timing.
- **Risk to mitigate:** Web Speech recognition occasionally misses
  the first phrase if the mic is cold. Do a "warmup" command before
  rolling the camera (say "test" once and discard).

### Capture F — Stacked worst-case profile (2:25–2:50)

- Onboarding page with all four axes set to a11y-active values
  (screen-reader / plain-language / dyslexia-friendly OR adhd-focus /
  slow). Hold 2 seconds so the audience reads the choices.
- Cut to the chat. Pre-recorded audio of VoiceOver announcing the
  live region — let *one* announcement land cleanly. (You can layer
  this over the music bed at higher volume so it cuts through.)
- Wide shot showing: Atkinson font, faded older bubbles, short tutor
  turns. Hold 3 seconds.
- 20–25 second clip.

### Capture G — Closing title card (2:50–3:00)

- Static graphic. Make this in Figma, Keynote, or a single React
  component if you prefer.
- "SapientIA" wordmark. Subtitle: "An inclusive Socratic tutor."
- Four small icons: eye (visual), brain (cognitive), book
  (learning), microphone (motor).
- "Built with Claude Opus 4.7" tagline at the bottom.
- 8–10 second hold. Fade to black.

## Recording tool stack

**Screen recording**

- **Mac built-in (Cmd+Shift+5)** — free, fast, captures system audio
  and microphone. Sufficient for hackathon demos. Default tool.
- **ScreenStudio** (paid, ~$89 one-time, Mac only) — adds zoom-on-
  click, smooth cursor animations, automatic captions. Worth it if
  you have multiple demos to record this year.
- **Loom** (free tier) — easy share links, picture-in-picture
  webcam. Good for the "hands off the keyboard" framing in Capture E.
- **OBS Studio** (free, all platforms) — overkill for a 3-minute
  demo, but handy if you want true multi-camera (screen + webcam +
  mic on separate tracks).

**Editing**

- **iMovie** (free, Mac) — sufficient for cut-paste-narrate. No
  fancy effects but does the job.
- **DaVinci Resolve** (free) — professional NLE if you want fine
  control over audio mixing, color grading, captions.
- **Descript** (paid, generous free tier) — text-driven editing
  ("delete this sentence" actually deletes the corresponding video).
  Pairs well with its own Overdub TTS.
- **CapCut** (free) — surprisingly capable, fast for social-media-
  style cuts. If you want a pacy edit, this is the easiest path.

**Captions**

- Auto-generate in Descript or CapCut, or upload to YouTube and
  download the auto-caption SRT. Always proofread; "SapientIA" will
  get mistranscribed as "Sapient AI" or "Sapientia" — fix manually.

## Production order (the actual workflow)

1. **Lock the script.** Read it aloud with a stopwatch. Trim ruthlessly
   if any scene runs long. Aim for 2:50, not 3:00 — you want a
   2-second buffer.
2. **Generate the voiceover** with ElevenLabs / OpenAI / Descript.
   Export as a single WAV file with the scene transitions baked in
   (or as 6 separate files, one per scene).
3. **Drop the voiceover into your editor first.** This is the
   skeleton. Every screen capture will be cut to fit it.
4. **Record screen captures one scene at a time.** Re-record any
   that look fumbled. Capture E (voice commands) usually needs 3+
   takes — budget for it.
5. **Lay captures over voiceover.** Trim each clip to match the
   narration beat. Cuts at sentence boundaries usually feel best.
6. **Add a music bed** at low volume (-20 dB or so). LoFi piano,
   ambient pad, anything non-distracting. Free options: YouTube Audio
   Library, Pixabay, Uppbeat. Avoid generic "tech demo" music — it
   makes the pitch sound like every other startup video.
7. **Add captions.** Burn them in or ship as a sidecar SRT file.
   Hackathon judges sometimes review with audio off.
8. **Render at 1080p, H.264, ≤ 100 MB.** Most submission portals cap
   uploads at 100 MB or less.
9. **Watch the final cut three times.** Once with audio. Once
   muted. Once at 0.75x speed. If any beat feels slow at 0.75x, it
   *was* slow at full speed — re-cut.

## What to leave OUT of the demo

The temptation in a technical hackathon is to show the technical
machinery. Resist it. None of these belong in the 3-minute pitch:

- The DebugPanel, soft violations, the pedagogy/violations table —
  fascinating internally, lost on a general audience.
- Code editor windows. Source code. Test results.
- The architecture diagram.
- Explanations of SSE streaming, prompt fragments, the tool schema.
- The terminal.
- Any mention of "LLM," "tool use," "system prompt," or "ADR."

If a judge wants to see those, they will read the README. The demo's
job is to make them *want* to read the README.
